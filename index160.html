BIBLE QUIZ: THE PRIVILEGE OF SUFFERING FOR CHRIST

Philippians 1:29
30
According to Philippians 1:29, it has been granted to you on behalf of Christ not only to believe in him, but also to ______ for him.
Serve
Suffer
Pray
Work

Correct Answer: Suffer (Philippians 1:29)

Philippians 1:29 (NIV): "For it has been granted to you on behalf of Christ not only to believe in him, but also to suffer for him."

The Divine Grant of Suffering:

  1. Dual Gift: Both faith and suffering are granted by God
  2. Christ-Centered: Suffering is specifically for Christ's sake
  3. Privilege Perspective: Suffering is a gift, not a curse
  4. Identification with Christ: Shares in His sufferings

Context in Philippians 1:27-30:

  • Call to live worthy of the gospel despite opposition
  • Standing firm in one spirit contending for the faith
  • Not being frightened by those who oppose you
  • Suffering as evidence of salvation for believers and condemnation for opponents
  • Same struggle the Philippians saw in Paul and now hear about him

What "Granted" (Echaristhē) Means:

  • Divine Gift: From the same word as "grace" (charis)
  • Privilege Language: Used for royal favors and honors
  • Not Punishment: Framed as a blessing, not a curse
  • Purposeful Suffering: Has meaning and divine purpose

The Twofold Grant:

  • To Believe: Faith itself is God's gift (Ephesians 2:8-9)
  • To Suffer: Persecution and hardship for Christ's name
  • Connected Experience: Genuine faith often leads to suffering
  • Complete Package: Both are part of the Christian calling

Biblical Theology of Suffering:

  • Christ's Example: He suffered first (1 Peter 2:21)
  • Apostolic Pattern: All apostles suffered for Christ
  • Fellowship with Christ: Sharing in His sufferings (Philippians 3:10)
  • Gospel Advance: Suffering often accompanies gospel spread
  • Future Glory: Present suffering vs eternal glory (Romans 8:18)

Practical Implications for Believers:

  • Changed Perspective: View suffering as privilege, not punishment
  • Courage in Witness: Willingness to face opposition for Christ
  • Identification with Christ: Deeper union through shared suffering
  • Eternal Perspective: Temporary suffering vs eternal reward
  • Community Support: Standing together in persecution

Modern Applications:

  • Religious Freedom Issues: Standing firm where Christianity is opposed
  • Social Pressure: Maintaining biblical values in secular culture
  • Workplace Witness: Facing discrimination for Christian principles
  • Family Opposition: Persevering when family rejects faith
  • Personal Sacrifice: Giving up comforts for gospel ministry

The Paradox of Christian Suffering:

  • Strength in Weakness: God's power perfected in our weakness
  • Joy in Sorrow: Rejoicing amid persecution (Acts 5:41)
  • Life through Death: Dying to self brings true life
  • Honor through Shame: Bearing disgrace for Christ's name

Key Insight: Christian suffering is not meaningless misfortune but a gracious gift from God that deepens our identification with Christ, strengthens our faith, advances the gospel, and prepares us for eternal glory. It's the surprising privilege that confirms our genuine faith and unites us with believers throughout history who have suffered for the name of Jesus.
